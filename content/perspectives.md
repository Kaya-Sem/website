There are two more, however; interobjective and intersubjective.

The concepts of "objective", "interobjective", "subjective" and "intersubjective" are often used in various fields such as philosophy, sociology and integral theory to describe different dimensions of reality or types of knowledge. Here's an overview of each:

1. Objective: This refers to aspects of reality that exist independently of human perception or beliefs. Objective reality can be measured and observed by multiple people, leading to similar results. Examples include scientific facts, physical laws, and empirical data.

2. Interobjective: This concept deals with the systems, relationships, and structures that exist between objects. It focuses on how objects interact and relate within a network or system. In integral theory, inerobjective reality refers to the shared systems and structures, such as ecosystems, economic systems, and social institutions, that shape our world and our interactions.

3. Subjective: Subjective reality is based on personal experiences, perceptions, and internal states. It encompasses individual feelings, thoughts, and interpretations. Subjective knowledge is inherently personal can vary significantly between individuals.

4. Intersubjective: This refers to the shared understanding and collective agreements among individuals within a culture or society. Intersubjective reality is created through communcation, language, and shared beliefs. Examples include cultural norms, social values, and common worldviews.


When referring to someone as "subjectively beautiful," it typically implies that beauty is based on individual preferences and perceptions. However, if beauty is considered within the context of a shared consensus or cultural agreement, it would be more accurate to describe it as "intersubjectively beautiful." Here’s a breakdown:

Let's take a look at a more concrete example:
*Subjectively Beautiful*: This means that an individual's perception of beauty is based on their personal preferences, feelings, and tastes. It’s a personal judgment that can vary widely from person to person.

*Intersubjectively Beautiful*: This implies that the perception of beauty is based on a collective or shared understanding within a group or culture. It acknowledges that while individual opinions may vary, there is a common agreement or cultural norm about what is considered beautiful.

Using "intersubjectively beautiful" highlights that beauty is not only a matter of individual taste but also shaped by social and cultural factors that lead to a consensus or shared standards of beauty. This distinction is important in recognizing the role of cultural norms and shared values in shaping our perceptions and judgments.
